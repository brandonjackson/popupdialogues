Pop-Up Dialogues

Start conversations in public spaces. Pop-Up Dialogues is a free toolkit of printable, customizable posters designed to get neighbors talking. Print one out and put it up.

Print

Print out a pop-up. The pop-ups are available in a variety of sizes and file formats.

Place

Place your pop-up in a public space like on a bulletin board, abandoned building or wall.

Talk

Watch as fellow citizens start conversations and share their opinions. Feel satisfied. Repeat.

Advice

Site Selection

Picking a place to hang your pop-up is one of the trickiest parts of staging an urban intervention. Common locations include public bulletin boards, on the walls of abandoned buildings, and on subway platforms. Here are some factors to consider:

The Law. Make sure you are familiar with the laws in your area regarding posters. It is often illegal to hang them without the permission of the property owners.

Traffic. Pop-ups in highly-trafficked areas will probably be more visible. However, it's also possible that citizens will be surprised to see installations in neglected areas and be more likely to engage.

Questions

Pop-Up Dialogues are designed to facilitate conversations, but ultimately it is up to you to decide what question to ask. The question depends on the kind of dialogue you want to inspire. Questions can get the pulse of the community on practical issues such as "Does this park need more trees?" Other pop-ups can be used to inspire conversations about the hopes and dreams of the community, such as this questions posed by artist Candy Chang: "Before I Die I Want To ______________".

Resources

Entrepreneurial citizens around the world are trying to improve the quality of public space through design and conversation. For those interested in getting connected with others involved in these projects, check out the work of Candy Chang and her New Orleans-based design studio, Civic Center. The non-profit organization Projects for Public Spaces has an excellent website filled with informative articles about things such as placemaking and community spaces GOOD and Next American City magazines are also very informative.
